# Chapter 2: Problem Statement

## 2.1 Challenges in Heritage Tourism
While tourism platforms are highly developed, the sub-sector of heritage tourism faces specific, systemic challenges that are unresolved by standard commercial tools. Heritage sites are fundamentally different from standard leisure destinations: they represent deep educational and historical value that cannot be captured by simple rating scores and restaurant listings. The lack of customized, context-sensitive guides leaves visitors feeling disconnected from the places they visit, reducing tourism to passive sightseeing instead of active cultural learning.

## 2.2 Lack of Centralized Heritage Information
Travelers who wish to learn about the deep history of a monument, temple, or historical city must search across fragmented, unverified sources. Commercial travel blogs are often repetitive, optimized for SEO rather than historical accuracy, and focus heavily on affiliate links. Conversely, official government archaeological databases are dry, academic, mobile-unfriendly, and lack practical tourist logistics like active opening hours, entry fees, and transportation routes. There is no single, centralized repository that cleanly binds official logistics, geographical mapping, current weather status, and rich architectural narratives into a single, cohesive interface.

## 2.3 Difficulty Planning Heritage Trips
Creating a travel itinerary for historical trips is uniquely challenging. A standard leisure itinerary might suggest a series of popular shopping malls or beaches. A heritage itinerary, however, requires sequencing monuments geographically to avoid excessive travel times, balancing historical exposure to prevent tourist fatigue, and aligning schedules with strict local factors (such as the temple closing for afternoon prayers, or the optimal time to catch the sunset from a hilltop fort). Manual trip planning is time-consuming, requiring travelers to cross-reference multiple maps, blogs, and logistics tables.

## 2.4 Poor Engagement with Historical Content
Traditional guides, audio tours, and informational plaque boards at heritage sites are static, rigid, and often dull. They do not adapt to the specific interests of the individual traveler. A family with children, an architecture student, and a casual backpacker are all shown the exact same block of text. This one-size-fits-all approach leads to low user engagement, rapid boredom, and a failure to convey the underlying historical and cultural value of the site.

## 2.5 Limited Access to Virtual Heritage Experiences
For individuals who cannot travel due to physical disabilities, financial constraints, or remote geographic locations, there is a total lack of high-fidelity, interactive, virtual heritage experiences. Most online tours are either expensive proprietary applications requiring VR headsets, or static, low-resolution photo slideshows. There is a critical need for an open-access, browser-native 360° virtual tour portal that embeds street-level panoramas directly alongside descriptive historical and logistical profiles of global landmarks.

## 2.6 Problems Faced by Travelers
In summary, travelers face a host of frustrating issues when exploring heritage sites:
* **Information Fragmentation:** Spending hours cross-referencing mapping APIs, travel portals, weather widgets, and cultural blogs.
* **Planning Bottlenecks:** Designing multi-day trips with geographical routes without understanding local travel guidelines.
* **Context Disconnect:** Standing in front of a thousand-year-old carving or temple spire without knowing its historical context or architectural significance.
* **Logistics Surprises:** Finding a site closed, being hit with unannounced entry fees, or being caught in sudden heavy rain without a weather warning.
* **Presentation Barriers:** Developer setups and presentation runs often fail due to strict redirect URLs and missing Google OAuth setups, limiting the utility of demonstration environments.
